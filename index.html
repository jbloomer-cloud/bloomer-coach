Core principles
🏃
Why 80/20 running
Zone 2 feels too easy. That's the point.
›
80% of your running volume at Zone 2. 20% at threshold or above. Not 50/50. Not 70/30.

Most amateur athletes run almost everything at a medium intensity — not easy enough to build aerobic base, not hard enough to improve lactate threshold. It feels productive because it's uncomfortable, but it sits in a physiological no-man's land that produces fatigue without the adaptation you're after.

Zone 2 (roughly 65–75% max HR, conversational pace) is where mitochondrial density increases, fat oxidation improves, and cardiac output builds. These are the engines that make everything else possible. You cannot shortcut this by running faster on your easy days — you just accumulate fatigue and suppress the adaptation signal.

The result: when the 20% hard work arrives (Wednesday quality session, strides, race pace intervals), your body is fresh, your aerobic ceiling is higher, and the quality is actually high quality. Running slowly on easy days is how you run fast on hard days.

Your Zone 2 pace right now is roughly 4:50–5:15/km. It will feel embarrassingly slow at first. By Week 16 your easy pace will have dropped 15–20 sec/km at the same heart rate. That is the adaptation working.

📈
Why sub-18 5k before the half marathon
Speed ceiling before volume ceiling.
›
Sub-18 5k requires a pace of 3:35/km. Sub-1:25 half marathon requires 4:02/km. Build the faster engine first.

There's a well-established principle in endurance sport: your race pace at longer distances is a fixed percentage of your maximum short-distance speed. If your 5k ceiling is 20:00, your half marathon won't go below approximately 1:35. Raise the 5k ceiling to 18:00 and the half becomes physiologically achievable at 1:25.

The opposite approach — grinding half marathon volume first — builds aerobic endurance but doesn't raise the speed ceiling. You become very good at running slowly for a long time. Training specifically for 5k speed develops VO2max, neuromuscular efficiency, and running economy in ways that long-distance training alone simply cannot.

What this means for your training: The Wednesday quality sessions in Phase 1 and 2 are specifically targeting 5k pace. Threshold intervals, VO2max work, and race-pace confidence runs. This is uncomfortable and feels like it contradicts the 80/20 principle — it doesn't. The 20% hard work is earning its place precisely because the 80% easy work is being done properly.

Why hypertrophy before strength
Build the tissue, then load the tissue.
›
Phase 1 gym: higher reps, moderate load, RPE 7–8. Phase 2: lower reps, heavier load, RPE 8–9. This is not arbitrary.

Hypertrophy (muscle growth) and strength (neural drive and force production) are related but distinct adaptations. Hypertrophy happens primarily in the 6–15 rep range at moderate-to-high RPE and creates the cross-sectional area of muscle that strength work then learns to recruit maximally.

Going straight into heavy strength work with an underdeveloped muscle belly limits how strong you can get. The connective tissue, tendons, and muscle fibres need a foundation period first. Building this base also reduces injury risk as load increases in Phase 2.

For a hybrid athlete: this matters even more because the gym has to coexist with significant running volume. Higher rep, lower absolute load in Phase 1 means less CNS fatigue overlap with the running work. By Phase 2, your aerobic base is established, your body is adapted to the training load, and you can push the heavy compounds hard without it compromising run quality.

The periodisation you'll feel: Phase 1 back squat might be 4×8 at 75kg. Phase 2 might be 5×4 at 95kg. That progression is only possible because Phase 1 built the tissue to support it.

🔄
Why deload weeks feel wrong but aren't
Adaptation happens during recovery, not during training.
›
Deload weeks: volume drops 40%, intensity stays. Weeks 2, 9, 15, 21 in your programme.

When you train, you create a stress response. Muscle fibres are damaged, glycogen is depleted, the nervous system is taxed. The adaptation — strength, speed, muscle growth — does not happen during the training. It happens in the 48–72 hours after, and more broadly across a recovery week.

If you never fully recover, you accumulate what sports scientists call residual fatigue. This masks fitness gains — you may be getting fitter but feel worse and perform worse because the fatigue is sitting on top of the adaptation. Deload weeks flush the fatigue and allow the accumulated adaptations to express themselves.

What you'll experience: after a deload week, the following week's sessions will feel noticeably better. Weights that felt heavy will feel lighter. Paces that felt hard will feel controlled. This is not you getting weaker during the deload — it is the adaptation you already earned becoming accessible.

The temptation is to train through deload weeks, especially when you're feeling good. Resist it. The reason you feel good is exactly why the deload is working. Use the week to sleep more, eat well, and come back to Week 10 or Week 16 ready to push properly.

🎯
Why core work is trained like a muscle
Stability is not the same as size or strength.
›
Weighted crunches, ab wheel rollouts, hanging leg raises — these are hypertrophy exercises. The plank is not.

Most gym programmes treat the core as a stabiliser only — planks, dead bugs, bird dogs. These are valuable but they don't build the rectus abdominis, obliques, and serratus the way direct loaded work does. If you want visible, functional core musculature, you need to train the abs the same way you train the chest — progressive overload, controlled eccentrics, full range of motion.

The ab wheel rollout is one of the highest-demand core exercises available. Full extension creates a long lever against which your abs must resist hyperextension — the eccentric load is significant. Starting from knees and progressing to standing is a meaningful strength journey.

Cable crunches and hanging leg raises are the direct loading movements. They train flexion under load, which is what creates thickness and definition. The anti-rotation work (pallof press, Copenhagen plank) is the functional complement — it trains the core's role in running economy and injury prevention.

The combination in your programme: Monday gets flexion + anti-rotation (weighted crunch + pallof press + ab wheel). Tuesday gets vertical flexion + stability (hanging leg raise + dead bug). Friday gets lateral stability + rotation (Copenhagen plank + Russian twist + loaded plank). Three planes, three sessions, progressive loading throughout the 24 weeks.

How to use RPE correctly
RPE 8 means 2 reps left in the tank. Not "pretty hard".
›
RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) on a 1–10 scale. RPE 10 = absolute maximum. RPE 8 = could do 2 more reps. RPE 6 = could do 4+ more.

RPE is the most honest way to prescribe intensity because it auto-regulates for daily fluctuation. A 100kg squat at RPE 8 on a rested day might be RPE 9 on a hard week. Percentage-based programming doesn't account for this — RPE does.

The common mistake is rating sessions as RPE 8 when they're actually RPE 6, either because the weight is too light or the reps are stopped too early. This is self-defeating — you get the fatigue without the adaptation signal.

A genuine RPE 8 set should end with you knowing you had 2 good reps left — not 5, not 1. The distinction matters. If you finish a set and immediately want to add more reps, it was probably RPE 6. If you're not sure you had another rep in you, that's RPE 9.

Progressive overload in this programme works as follows: when you consistently hit the top of the rep range (e.g. 7 reps on a 5–7 set) at RPE 8, add 2.5kg to upper body movements and 5kg to lower body. Do not add weight just because the session felt okay — add it because you demonstrably hit the ceiling of the rep range with good form.

How to find your real lactate threshold
Your pace zones are estimates. A field test makes them precise.
›
LT2 field test: 30 min maximal time trial. Average HR of the final 20 minutes = your LT2 heart rate. All threshold work is then based on this number.

Your current pace zones are calculated from a 5k estimate using standard formulae. These are accurate within about 10–15% — good enough to start, but not precise. Individual physiology varies significantly. Some athletes have a lactate threshold at 85% of max HR, others at 90%. The formula cannot know which you are.

The 30-minute time trial is the gold standard field test. Run on a flat route or track. Go as hard as you can sustain for 30 minutes — not a sprint, but a sustained maximal effort. Note your average heart rate for the final 20 minutes. That HR is your LT2. Your threshold running pace is then the pace you held for those 20 minutes.

When to test: do it at Week 6 (when you have some aerobic base but before the quality sessions ramp). Repeat at Week 14 to see the adaptation. The improvement in pace at the same LT2 heart rate over 8 weeks is one of the most direct measures of aerobic development you can get without a lab.

Update your pace zones in the Benchmarks section after every test. The difference between estimated and actual zones can change your Wednesday session from "easy" to "exactly right" — and that difference compounds over 18 weeks of training.

Running volume vs lifting — the trade-off you need to understand
Above 40km/wk, running suppresses muscle growth.
›
Endurance training above ~40km/wk elevates cortisol and activates AMPK, which directly inhibits mTOR — the primary anabolic signalling pathway for muscle growth.

This is not a reason to run less — it is a reason to be honest about what you can achieve simultaneously. You can maintain muscle mass and improve running performance at the same time. You cannot maximise both simultaneously. The programme prioritises performance over aesthetics in Phase 2 and 3, and that is the correct prioritisation for your goals.

Practical implications: in Phase 3, when weekly run volume hits 45–55km, reduce gym sets by 20–25% and accept that hypertrophy gains will plateau. This is not failure — it is correct periodisation. The muscle you built in Phase 1 is the foundation; Phase 3 is about maintaining it while the running volume peaks.

Nutrition becomes the primary lever when volume is high. Adequate protein (2g/kg non-negotiable) and caloric surplus on hard training days can partially offset the anabolic suppression from endurance volume. Eating in a deficit while running 50km/wk will result in muscle loss regardless of how well the gym programme is structured.

The honest number: for someone with your goals and your training age, expect to add 1–2kg of lean mass over the 24 weeks while improving your 5k by 10%+ and your half marathon by 5–8 minutes. That is an excellent result for concurrent training. The athletes who get frustrated are the ones who expect gym-only hypertrophy rates alongside Hyrox-level running volume. Physics does not allow it.

Nutrition periodisation — eating differently on different days
Flat macros across 7 days is suboptimal. Day-specific fuelling changes everything.
›
Monday and Friday are heavy lift days — caloric surplus, front-loaded carbs. Wednesday is quality run day — high carb. Sunday is rest — protein-focused, lower calories.

The logic is straightforward: your body needs different fuels for different work, and providing those fuels at the right time improves both performance and body composition simultaneously. This is not complicated — it is applying the same periodisation logic to nutrition that you already apply to training.

Heavy lift days (Mon, Fri): 300–320g carbs, 2,900–3,100 kcal. Pre-session: 40–60g fast carbs 45 minutes before. Post-session: 50g protein + 80g carbs within 30 minutes.

Quality run day (Wed): 300g+ carbs, prioritise the morning. Gel or 30g fast carbs 20 minutes before the session. No heavy fat intake pre-run.

Easy days (Tue AM run, Thu): 250–270g carbs. These days support recovery — no need to spike calories, but do not under-eat.

Rest day (Sun): 190–210g carbs, higher fats, maintain protein at 156g+. The common mistake is over-eating on rest days or severely under-eating — both impair the recovery the rest day is supposed to provide.

Long run day (Sat): 350–380g carbs. Eat the night before. Gel or chews every 20–25 minutes if over 60 minutes running. Post-run: the largest meal of the week. Do not skip this.

Why wall balls are a genuine fitness test
Not a conditioning exercise — a coordination, power, and endurance diagnostic.
›
Wall balls require: full-depth squat strength, explosive hip and leg drive, upper body power to project the ball, coordination to catch and reload, and the aerobic capacity to sustain all of this for 100 reps in a race.

The wall ball breaks most hybrid athletes because it sits at an uncomfortable intersection — it is too technical to be purely a conditioning exercise, and too metabolically demanding to be purely a skill exercise. Your quads, glutes, shoulders, and lungs all fail at different times, and the one that fails first determines your limiting factor.

In your Hyrox, weakness on wall balls most likely came from one of two things: quad fatigue from the sled stations arriving before the wall ball station, or insufficient hip drive meaning your shoulders were compensating for what your legs should be doing. Both are fixable.

The training fix: the goblet squat thruster (squat deep, drive up explosively, press the weight overhead at the top) trains the exact triple extension pattern of a wall ball without a wall. If you can do 20 heavy goblet squat thrusters without the upper body failing, your wall balls will improve. The conditioning blocks in Monday and Friday now include wall balls specifically — 5 rounds of 15 reps is approximately the stimulus you need per session to see race-relevant improvement over 8 weeks.

What to focus on in technique: the ball should leave your hands at the top of the squat drive, not be pushed with your arms. If your arms are doing the work, your legs are not loaded correctly. Stand close to the wall — about an arm-length away. Catch the ball at chest height and descend into the squat immediately. The rhythm is the skill.

Why lunges are a Hyrox priority
The lunge is not a leg exercise. It is a hip extension and deceleration exercise.
›
The Hyrox walking lunge (Farmer Lunge — 200m with sandbag) fails athletes for one reason: the hip flexors and glutes cannot sustain the full extension-to-flexion cycle at race pace under load for 200 metres.

Most gym lunges are too short, too slow, and too upright to transfer to Hyrox. The Hyrox lunge requires a long stride, full hip extension at the top (glute firing completely), and controlled deceleration of the front leg — all while carrying load and already fatigued from previous stations.

The walking lunge in your conditioning blocks is programmed specifically to address this. The cue that matters most is hip extension at the top — most athletes step forward and immediately lunge again without ever fully straightening. That incomplete extension is what accumulates fatigue fastest. Drive the hips through fully before stepping the rear foot forward.

Progressive loading: start the lunge conditioning with bodyweight or light DBs. The goal is not to go heavy — it is to sustain quality over volume. 20 lunges per leg in a conditioning block is significantly more Hyrox-relevant than 8 heavy barbell lunges in a strength set. Both have a place, but for Hyrox improvement, volume at quality beats load.

Why it connects to the sled: the lunge and the sled pull are mechanically similar — both demand hip extension under load, with the same posterior chain muscles doing the primary work. Improving your lunge improves your sled pull, and vice versa. They are not separate weaknesses. They are the same weakness expressed in two different patterns.

Why core gets its own dedicated block every session
Core trained as an afterthought produces an afterthought core.
›
Core work is placed at the end of sessions but is protected in short session mode. It does not get cut. This is deliberate and the reason is specific.

Every gym programme deprioritises core when time is tight. The compound lifts take the available time and the core block gets dropped. Over a training cycle, this means the athlete gets stronger in their primary movements but the stability and strength that underpins all of them stagnates. For a hybrid athlete, this has a direct consequence: running economy, sled push position, wall ball coordination, and overhead stability all depend on core strength in ways that are not obvious until they fail under fatigue.

The core programme in your plan covers three planes of motion across three sessions. Monday trains sagittal flexion and anti-rotation (weighted crunch, pallof press, ab wheel rollout — direct rectus abdominis and oblique hypertrophy). Tuesday trains vertical flexion and lumbar stability (hanging leg raise, dead bug — hip flexor strength and lumbar spine control). Friday trains lateral stability and rotation (Copenhagen plank, Russian twist, weighted plank with reach — frontal and transverse plane).

Why this matters for Hyrox specifically: the sled push requires your spine to resist extension under load as you drive. Wall balls require your midline to stay rigid at the catch and through the squat. The lunge requires your pelvis to stay level with each stride. All three weaknesses you identified have a core stability component. A strong, well-trained core does not just look better — it makes every Hyrox station more efficient because less energy is lost to positional breakdown.

Progressive overload in core training: the same principle applies as any other muscle — increase load or difficulty over time. Ab wheel from knees progresses to from feet. Pallof press increases cable resistance weekly. Copenhagen plank increases hold time. Russian twist increases weight plate load. If you are doing the same core exercises at the same load you started with in Week 3, you are maintaining, not developing. Treat core like any other training priority.

Why core is non-negotiable and never gets skipped
Core weakness shows up in every Hyrox station. It just looks like something else.
›
Every gym programme deprioritises core when time is tight. Compounds take the available time and the core block gets dropped. This is protected in this programme — core groups are never removed in short session mode.

The sled push requires your spine to resist extension under load as you drive. Wall balls require your midline to stay rigid at the catch and through the squat. The lunge requires your pelvis to stay level with each stride. The ski erg demands anti-rotation stability on every pull. All three of your Hyrox weaknesses have a core stability component that is not obvious until you are fatigued at station 5 and your position collapses.

The three planes being trained: Monday hits sagittal plane flexion (weighted crunch) and anti-rotation (pallof press + ab wheel rollout). Tuesday hits vertical flexion under load (hanging leg raise) and contralateral stability (dead bug). Friday hits the frontal plane (Copenhagen plank) and rotational strength (Russian twist). You are not doing random core work — you are systematically training every plane of motion that fails in race conditions.

Ab wheel rollout specifically: this is the highest-demand core exercise in the programme. Full extension creates a long lever against which your abs must resist hyperextension — the eccentric load is significant and directly transfers to the positional demands of the sled push. Progress from knees to full standing rollout. When you can do 12 clean standing rollouts, your core will not be the limiting factor in any Hyrox station.

What neglected core actually looks like in a race: lower back fatigue on the sled. Hips rising on the sled push. Upper body leaning excessively on the lunge. Losing ball height on wall balls in the final 20 reps. These are not strength failures — they are stability failures. Core work fixes them.

How Hyrox-specific work fits into a hypertrophy programme
The H groups and conditioning are not optional extras. They are the bridge between gym strength and race performance.
›
The principle: gym strength is potential. Hyrox-specific work converts that potential into race performance. You need both — one without the other leaves a gap that shows up on race day.

The research on specificity in strength training is consistent: adaptations are specific to the movement patterns, velocities, and loads used in training. A heavy back squat builds quad and glute strength in a vertical pattern. That strength does not automatically transfer to a sled push because the sled push is a horizontal pattern. The H groups (wall drives, resisted split squats, goblet thrusters, cable pull-throughs) are the specificity bridge — they use the strength the main compounds build, and apply it to the exact patterns that Hyrox demands.

The programming logic: main compounds (A and B groups) build the strength base. Accessory work (C, D, E groups) develops the supporting musculature and corrects imbalances. The H groups convert strength to Hyrox-specific performance. The conditioning blocks train the energy system under race conditions. Each layer depends on the one below it — you cannot programme-hop between H groups and conditioning without the compound base underneath.

Phase 1 priority: in the hypertrophy block, the H groups are moderate intensity — they train the pattern and build the tissue, but they do not override the hypertrophy focus. Phase 2 is where the H groups intensify alongside the strength work. By Phase 3 (HM block), the Hyrox-specific work maintains the patterns while running volume peaks. The race-readiness for your next Hyrox is being built now, even though the event is months away.
